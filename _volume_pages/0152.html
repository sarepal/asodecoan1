{% raw %}The Professional Ideal of the Dental Surgeon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(Having many times read and re-read the Oath and the Law of Hippocrates, that noble{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and celebrated Greek physician of twenty-three centuries ago, I attempt here to scan a few{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lines along this line of thought and principle, thinking it good for those who are about to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}enter the Profession.){% endraw %}
{% raw %}MEDICINE is of all the arts the most noble; of its many branches the art{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of dental surgery stands in the foreground in rank and file with general{% endraw %}
{% raw %}medicine and surgery. But this is only recently so. In the past, owing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the ignorance and narrow training of those who practiced it, and of those who,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inconsiderately, form a judgment of them, it wras far behind all the other arts. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}art is no longer one solely of mechanics, plugging in fillings and the like, but a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}high and noble art, dealing with vital tissues and general health of mankind, de¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}manding of those who practice it a thorough practical knowledge and understand¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing of the anatomy and pathology thereof.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Now that dental surgery is, and righteously so, on a high and unimpeachable{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plane, may those who practice it be competent and respectful men and ever diligent{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and faithful of the noble art they are practicing.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}"Inexperience is a bad treasure, and a bad fund to those who possess it,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wdiether in opinion or reality, being devoid of self-reliance and contentedness, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the nurse both of timidity and audacity. For timidity betrays a want of powers,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and audacity a lack of skill. They are, indeed, two things, knowledge and opin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ion, of which the one makes its possessor really to know, and the other to be ignor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ant." Thus said Hoppocrates.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Be conscientious and thorough in all your work. Follow that system of regimen{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, according to your ability and judgment, you consider for the benefit of your{% endraw %}
{% raw %}patients, and abstain from whatever is deleterious and mischievous.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}With purity and holiness pass your life and practice your art.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}While you faithfully continue to keep and practice these principles, may it be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}granted to you to enjoy life and the practice of the art, respected and esteemed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}all men, in all times; but should you be not faithful in this respect, may the reverse{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be your lot.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}"Life is short, and the art long; the occasion fleeting; experience fallacious,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and judgment difficult."{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The dental surgeon must not only be prepared to do what is right himself, but{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also to make the patient, and the attendants co-operate.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Henry M. Needham.{% endraw %}
